Executive Summary

"Traditional regulatory bans often lead to black markets and high enforcement costs. Economic incentives turn environmental cleanup into a revenue-generating opportunity for municipal development."
— Principles of Ecological Economics

Philippine cities and municipalities face accelerating environmental crises: clogged waterways causing severe urban flooding, escalating garbage hauling fees, and deteriorating urban air quality. Traditional "Command-and-Control" (CAC) regulations—such as outright bans on plastics or vehicle restrictions—are difficult to enforce, often place severe economic burdens on micro-enterprises, and drain limited LGU resources.

This Policy Brief introduces Market-Based Instruments (MBIs) as a fiscally responsible and highly effective alternative. Based on the principles of Ecological Economics, which models the economy as a subsystem of the Earth’s finite ecosystem, these tools "internalize" environmental damages directly into market prices.

Generate Green Revenue

Fees and levies go directly to a local trust fund earmarked for municipal environmental projects.

Reduce Enforcement Strain

Economic incentives leverage self-interest, causing businesses and residents to naturally reduce waste.

Promote Clean Jobs

Revenue from local fees can directly fund green livelihoods, upland forest wardens, and recycling facilities.

LGU Legal Authority: Section 186 of the Local Government Code of 1991 (R.A. 7160) explicitly empowers LGUs to create their own sources of revenue, including environmental user fees, carbon offsets, and specific local levies, provided they are not unjust, excessive, or contrary to national policy.

The Problem: Market Failures in PH Cities

In standard neoclassical economics, environmental pollution is treated as a negative externality—a cost suffered by a third party (the general public, downstream ecosystems, or future generations) as a consequence of an economic transaction. Because polluters do not pay this cost, they produce too much pollution.

Under ecological economics, we analyze two pressing environmental failures currently dragging down local municipal budgets in the Philippines:

Solid Waste and the "Tingi" Economy

The Philippines consumes approximately **163 million single-use plastic sachets** daily. Due to the "tingi" culture, low-income households buy goods in micro-portions. The cost of packaging and disposal is not included in the sachet price. LGUs bear the cost of cleanup, landfill tipping fees (ranging from **PHP 600 to PHP 1,200 per ton**), and drainage declogging. This represents a massive subsidy from public tax funds to plastic manufacturers.

Urban Air Pollution and Healthcare Costs

Particulate Matter (PM2.5) levels in Metro Manila, Cebu, and urban centers frequently exceed WHO safety standards. The health costs of respiratory illnesses (asthma, COPD, cardiovascular diseases) are borne by the public healthcare system (PhilHealth and municipal health offices) and individual families, while vehicle operators pay nothing for the air degradation they cause.

The Market-Based Policy Toolbox

Rather than dictating behavior, market-based tools use price signals to align private incentives with social and environmental goals. We recommend two major tools easily implementable by LGUs:

01

Pigouvian Taxes (Local Eco-Fees)

Highly Feasible

A Pigouvian tax is a tax levied on any market activity that generates negative externalities. The tax is set equal to the marginal external cost of the damage, correcting the market price.

Philippine Application: LGUs can levy a **"Point-of-Sale Eco-Fee"** (e.g., PHP 2.00 to PHP 5.00) on single-use plastic carry-out bags (similar to ordinances in Quezon City and Pasig City). Retailers collect the fee, which is deposited directly into the city's Green Fund, rather than absorbed as corporate profit.
Pigouvian Formula: Price_{Social} = Price_{Private} + Tax_{Pigouvian}
02

Local Carbon Offsets & Cap-and-Trade

Medium Complexity

A cap-and-trade system sets a maximum level (cap) on pollution and issues tradable allowances. Polluters who exceed their allowances must buy credits from those who emit less.

Philippine Application: Large commercial buildings (malls, high-rises) and industrial plants must match a percentage of their carbon footprint by purchasing local **"Municipal Blue Carbon/Green Carbon Credits."** The LGU sells these credits to fund local reforestation (e.g., restoring watershed forests in upland areas or planting mangroves in coastal zones). This creates a direct market connection between urban emitters and rural/coastal conservationists.
Offset Formula: Carbon_{Emitted} - Offset_{Purchased} \le Cap_{Allowed}

Legal & Policy Implementation Roadmap

Transitioning to market-based policy tools requires a systematic legal approach to ensure ordinances are not challenged in court. Below is the step-by-step roadmap for Philippine LGUs under **R.A. 7160**.

1

Technical Study & Baseline Assessment

Conduct a local waste audit (WACS) or carbon inventory. Identify the marginal cost of pollution to set the Pigouvian fee correctly (e.g., matching local landfill cost per bag).

2

Public Consultation & Stakeholder Dialogues

Present the proposed fees to business councils, supermarket chains, chamber of commerce, and civil society groups. Frame the fee not as a tax, but as an **environmental service user charge**.

3

Drafting the LGU Ordinance

Submit the ordinance to the Sanggunian (Council) Environment Committee. Define explicit earmarking rules so that all collected revenue enters a **Special Trust Account for the Environment**.

4

Enforcement & Monitoring

Deploy the Business Permits and Licensing Office (BPLO) to inspect retail point-of-sales. Setup a public dashboard to show how LGU Green Funds are being spent (e.g., number of trees planted, sorting centers built) to maintain public trust.

SANGGUNIANG PANLUNGSOD

Office of the Secretary to the Sanggunian

City of Liloan, Province of Cebu

ORDINANCE NO. ____, SERIES OF 2026
AN ORDINANCE CREATING AN ENVIRONMENTAL RECOVERY TRUST FUND, LEVYING AN ECO-FEE ON SINGLE-USE PLASTICS, AND ESTABLISHING A LOCAL CARBON COMPENSATION SYSTEM IN THE CITY OF LILOAN.

WHEREAS, Section 16 of Republic Act No. 7160, otherwise known as the Local Government Code of 1991, mandates all Local Government Units to ensure and promote the health and safety of their inhabitants, and enhance the right of the people to a balanced and healthful ecology;

WHEREAS, Section 186 of R.A. No. 7160 grants LGUs the authority to levy taxes, fees, or charges on any base or subject not otherwise specifically enumerated or taxed under national laws;

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT ORDAINED by the Sangguniang Panlungsod of Liloan, in session assembled, that:

SECTION 1. TITLE.

This Ordinance shall be known as the "LGU Sustainability Incentives Act of 2026."

SECTION 2. REGULATORY POINT-OF-SALE PLASTIC BAG FEE.

There is hereby levied an Environmental Eco-Fee of PHP 3.00 on every single-use plastic bag provided to consumers at any point of sale in commercial establishments, supermarkets, department stores, and fast-food chains within the municipal boundaries.

SECTION 3. LOCAL CARBON COMPENSATORY OFFSET TARIFF.

All category-A commercial developments, warehouses, and industrial structures operating within the LGU shall compensate for their estimated scope-2 carbon emissions by paying a local carbon offset tariff of PHP 500.00 per metric ton of CO₂ equivalent, unless said businesses purchase equivalent municipal forestry/mangrove carbon credits.

SECTION 4. CREATION OF SPECIAL TRUST ACCOUNT.

All revenues collected under Sections 2 and 3 of this Ordinance shall be deposited directly into a designated LGU Special Trust Account for the Environment, which shall be audit-segregated from the general fund and utilized solely for local reforestation, recycling facility operations, and clean energy projects.

SECTION 5. SEPARABILITY CLAUSE.

If any provision of this Ordinance is declared unconstitutional or invalid, the other provisions not affected thereby shall continue in full force and effect.
